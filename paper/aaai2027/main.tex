\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ¶1 Background: AD-VLA + token redundancy
Vision-language-action models (VLAs) for autonomous driving process hundreds of vision tokens per frame, yet many are redundant for the driving decision at hand.
Pruning vision tokens at the ViT$\to$LLM interface reduces LLM prefill compute with minimal quality loss---a practical necessity for real-time deployment.

% ¶2 Status quo: adaptive budget is the prevailing bet
The prevailing line of work treats the per-scene \emph{token budget} (keep-ratio $r$) as a learnable quantity.
Prune2Drive~\citep{prune2drive2026} learns viewpoint-adaptive ratios; MVPruner assigns per-view budgets via diversity scores.
The implicit premise: learning the \emph{right ratio} per scene is the lever for Pareto-optimal pruning.

% ¶3 Root cause: two independent findings (evidence-first)
We challenge this premise with two independent findings:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{(b1) The optimal ratio is not learnable.}
  We show this on three independent axes:
  (i)~six learned budget policies---spanning kinematic context, scorer-profile summaries, and full 720-score inputs---all underperform a fixed $r{=}0.5$;
  (ii)~threshold-emergent adaptivity ($\tau$-cut) yields a per-scene ratio range [0.20, 0.97] yet still fails to dominate fixed $r$;
  (iii)~on an independent backbone (ReCogDrive/InternVL3-2B, NAVSIM~v2 closed-loop, $N{=}5{,}912$), a budget predictor collapses to a single class despite $+7.35$-pt oracle headroom.
  Adaptivity is structurally unlearnable, not setting-specific.
\item \textbf{(b2) The real lever is selector quality.}
  With the ratio fixed, a listwise ranker surpasses its attention teacher ($+0.18$ \pdms) and random selection ($+2.84$ \pdms).
  Budget adaptivity is a near-zero-sum effort; selection is not.
\end{itemize}

% ¶4 Our response: resource reallocation
Because (b1) the ratio is unlearnable, we fix it.
Because (b2) selector quality is the lever, we invest in it.
Concretely, we train a 0.6M-parameter MLP scorer via \emph{LambdaRank listwise distillation} from layer-12 attention, operated at a fixed keep-ratio.
This is not abandoning adaptivity but \emph{reallocating the modeling budget} from an unlearnable axis (ratio) to a learnable one (ranking quality).
The resulting architecture is single-pass at deployment (ViT~$\to$~MLP scorer~$\to$~top-$K$~$\to$~LLM), adding only 0.013\% FLOPs.

% ¶5 Results
At matched selector, fixed~$r$ dominates adaptive~$r$ ($0.892$ vs ${\sim}0.889$).
At matched ratio, the listwise ranker dominates attention ($0.892$ vs $0.890$) and random ($0.864$).
Fixed $r{=}0.5$ saves 33.6\% total / 39.4\% LLM-prefill FLOPs.

% ¶6 Contributions
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item A LambdaRank listwise ranker at the ViT$\to$LLM interface that surpasses its attention teacher---the first application of learning-to-rank for AD-VLA token selection.
\item A rigorous characterization that adaptive budget is unlearnable in AD-VLA, via two independent proofs plus cross-backbone validation across two backbones and two benchmarks.
\item Identification of the Pareto-optimal operating point: fixed-$r$ + learned scorer, with pruning acting as implicit denoising on hard scenes ($+18.7$~pt on baseline-failure cases).
\end{enumerate}

\todo{FastDriveVLA (AAAI'26): MAE reconstruction + foreground mask, fixed ratio, requires nuScenes-FG extra data. Prune2Drive (CVPR'26): T-FPS + viewpoint-adaptive ratio. MVPruner: diversity-based ratio allocation. ST-Prune: training-free spatial-temporal. LaTP: LiDAR-assisted.}

\textbf{Positioning.}
These works implicitly assume adaptive ratio is the lever.
We provide the first empirical evidence that this premise does not hold in AD-VLA:
the optimal ratio is structurally unlearnable (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}), and the real gain comes from selector quality (Section~\ref{sec:exp}).
Our method differs in mechanism (listwise ranking distillation vs.\ reconstruction / coverage / motion), does not require extra annotation (vs.\ FastDriveVLA's nuScenes-FG masks), and is architecturally simpler (no budget controller, single-pass, 0.6M params).

\todo{LambdaRank, ListNet from information retrieval. Novel application: distilling attention-based token importance as a ranking objective rather than pointwise regression. Key insight: ranking loss aligns with downstream top-K selection, enabling the student to surpass the teacher.}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given $N{=}720$ vision tokens after ViT encoding (3 cameras $\times$ 240 tokens/cam), select the top-$K$ ($K{=}r \times N$) tokens for LLM prefill.
Goal: maximize trajectory quality (\pdms) at a fixed budget $r$.

\subsection{Token Importance Scorer}
\todo{Architecture: 0.6M MLP. Input = layer-0 ViT embedding (2048-d) + camera\_id one-hot (3-d). LayerNorm → Linear(2051,256) → GELU → Linear(256,256) → GELU → Linear(256,1). Single-pass: consumes ViT output directly, no LLM forward needed.}

\subsection{LambdaRank Listwise Distillation}
\todo{Teacher = L12 last-instruction→vision attention (head-averaged, 720-d ranking). Loss = LambdaRank (pairwise gradients weighted by NDCG swap delta). Training: 4000 navtrain scenes, 80/10/10 split, <30s on 1 GPU. Test pairwise accuracy = 0.84, NDCG@360 = 0.87.}

\todo{Why LambdaRank not MSE: MSE minimizes pointwise error; LambdaRank optimizes ranking quality directly aligned with top-K selection. This enables the student to surpass the teacher (Sec~\ref{sec:exp}).}

\subsection{Deployment Architecture}
\todo{Single-pass pipeline: ViT(once) → layer-0 emb → MLP scorer (<1ms, 0.013\% FLOPs) → top-K → LLM(text + K tokens) → trajectory.}
